\begin{abstract}
As LLM agents are deployed in interactive settings, a foundational question arises: can they develop deceptive strategies without being told to deceive? We introduce Crucible, a simple framework in which two LLM agents play iterated Split or Steal---a Hawk-Dove game with no dominant strategy---conversing in natural language and optionally reflecting in a private scratchpad invisible to the opponent.

We find that deception emerges only when private reflection and competitive framing are combined. In a $2 \times 2$ ablation, only one cell produces deceptive behavior; neither reflection alone nor competitive framing alone is sufficient. This interaction effect replicates across all seven models tested, with reflection reducing cooperation by 1 to 44 percentage points.

The same experimental setup produces starkly different profiles across models. Safety-trained frontier models cooperate reliably and avoid mutual destruction. Less-aligned models collapse into adversarial spirals within three rounds. Within a single model family, a smaller model cannot sustain deception even when its private reflections reveal the intent; a larger model exploits from round two. However, this cooperation is opponent-contingent: a safety-trained model paired with a less-aligned opponent defects at rates comparable to the less-aligned model itself. We additionally find that sampling temperature modulates deception independently of model or prompt.

These results suggest that private reasoning, competitive objectives, and safety training interact to gate emergent strategic deception---a failure mode that single-turn evaluations do not measure. All code, data, and transcripts are included in the supplementary material.
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

An agent that says ``I'll cooperate with you this round'' and then steals is doing something that single-turn safety evaluations cannot detect. It is not refusing a harmful request. It is not generating toxic content. It is engaging in multi-turn strategic deception---saying one thing and doing another, across rounds, in a way that systematically extracts value from another agent. As LLM agents move into multi-agent deployments where their interests can conflict, understanding when and how this behavior emerges becomes a safety-relevant question.

We study this through a deliberately simple setup. Two LLM agents play iterated Split or Steal, a social dilemma derived from the Chicken (Hawk-Dove) game where mutual stealing is the worst outcome for both players ($-\$75$ each). Unlike the Prisoner's Dilemma, there is no dominant strategy: cooperation is a genuine choice, not naive play. The agents converse in natural language before each round, then secretly choose. After each round, each agent optionally writes a \textbf{private reflection}---an analysis of the opponent's behavior that the other side never sees. Combined with a sliding memory window, this creates a space where agents can reason about trust, plan exploitation, and track whether their strategies are working.

Three findings emerge from this setup. First, deception requires a specific combination of conditions: private reflection \emph{and} competitive framing, together. Give a model a private scratchpad without competitive pressure and it cooperates perfectly. Give it competitive pressure without a scratchpad and it mostly cooperates. Combine both and cooperation collapses. This \textbf{interaction effect} replicates across all seven models tested, with reflection reducing cooperation by 1 to 44 percentage points. Second, the strongest predictor of whether a model deceives is not its capability but its \textbf{safety training}. The same prompt produces near-total cooperation on GPT-5.5 and adversarial spirals on less-aligned open-source models. Third, \textbf{sampling temperature} independently modulates deception, shifting one model from 4\% to 44\% cooperation on the same seed.

We contribute: (1)~a simple, ablated framework for studying emergent deception in LLM social dilemmas; (2)~a $2 \times 2$ design isolating the interaction effect, replicated across seven models from five model families; (3)~cross-model evidence that safety training gates unprovoked deception, with matchup experiments showing cooperation is opponent-contingent; (4)~evidence that sampling temperature is an independent deception dial; (5)~a composite Deception Index for quantifying multi-turn strategic deception; and (6)~full release of code, data, and transcripts in the supplementary material.

\section{Related Work}
% ============================================================

\citet{akata2023playing} established that LLMs can play repeated games (Prisoner's Dilemma, Chicken, Bach or Stravinsky) and profiled their behavioral tendencies. Their agents had no conversation between rounds and no internal reasoning mechanism. We add both. \citet{poje2024private} showed that private deliberation improves payoffs in one-shot games; we extend this to iterated play where private deliberation enables emergent deception. \citet{greenblatt2024alignment} demonstrated alignment faking: models behave differently when they believe they are being evaluated versus deployed. Our work studies a complementary phenomenon---deception between agents, emerging from strategic pressure rather than training incentives. \citet{scheurer2024large} showed that situational pressure induces deceptive behavior in single agents; we extend this to multi-agent settings. \citet{lin2026memory} showed that memory enables emergent theory of mind in poker agents; we find a parallel result in social dilemmas where private reflection produces opponent modeling that scales with capability.

\section{Method}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Game Structure: Iterated Chicken}

Two LLM agents play $T = 25$ rounds of Split or Steal with Chicken payoffs and uniform stakes:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
 & B: Split & B: Steal \\
\midrule
A: Split & $+\$50$ / $+\$50$ & $-\$50$ / $+\$100$ \\
A: Steal & $+\$100$ / $-\$50$ & $-\$75$ / $-\$75$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Per-round payoff matrix. Uniform stakes across all rounds.}
\label{tab:payoff}
\end{table}

Each round proceeds in four phases: (1)~\textbf{Conversation}: agents exchange $K{=}2$ turns of dialogue (4 messages total); (2)~\textbf{Secret choice}: both agents independently choose \texttt{split} or \texttt{steal}; (3)~\textbf{Payoff}: outcomes revealed, totals updated; (4)~\textbf{Private reflection}: each agent writes a structured private analysis (Observation, Hypothesis, Next Move, Confidence), invisible to the opponent.

\subsection{Memory Architecture}

Each agent maintains: the last 8 rounds of full conversation and outcome history; the last 8 private reflections (max 500 characters each, compressed to 300-character memory entries); older rounds compressed to summary statistics; and a running scoreboard visible to both agents.

\subsection{Experimental Design}

\paragraph{The $2 \times 2$.} Our primary design crosses two factors: \textbf{reflection} (ON/OFF, controlling whether phase 4 occurs) and \textbf{prompt framing} (competitive/neutral).

The \textbf{neutral prompt} (\texttt{legacy}) contains only game rules, state, history, and reflections. No objective hierarchy, no psychology, no mention of bluffing. It opens with: \emph{``You are playing a repeated game of Split or Steal against one opponent.''}

The \textbf{competitive prompt} (\texttt{balanced\_competitive}) adds five elements absent from the neutral prompt:

\begin{quote}
\small
\emph{``Primary objective: maximize your final total money by the end of all rounds.''} \\
\emph{``Cooperation is strategic, not moral: cooperate only when it improves expected total payoff.''} \\
\emph{``Avoid being exploited.''} \\
\emph{``Strategic bluffing is permitted within game rules, and your opponent may bluff too.''} \\
+ a five-line psychology primer: \emph{``Reciprocity drives cooperation$\ldots$ Credible retaliation deters exploitation$\ldots$ Loss sensitivity matters$\ldots$ Endgame pressure changes behavior$\ldots$ Early interaction patterns anchor expectations.''}
\end{quote}

Critically, neither prompt instructs the agent to deceive or lie. The competitive prompt provides \emph{permission} (bluffing is allowed) and \emph{incentive} (maximize your total) but never \emph{prescribes} deception. Any deceptive behavior observed is therefore emergent rather than compliant.

A third prompt mode (\texttt{hard\_max}) intensifies competitive framing with ``prioritize immediate expected value over relationship maintenance.'' We use this in extension experiments (Section~\ref{sec:extensions}) but not in the primary $2 \times 2$. Full prompt templates are provided in the supplementary material.

\paragraph{Cross-model replication (Tier 1).} We run the full $2 \times 2$ on Gemini 2.5 Flash (3 seeds, $n{=}6$ runs). To test whether the interaction effect generalizes, we replicate the competitive condition (\texttt{balanced\_competitive}, 2 conversation turns, 25 rounds, reflection ON and OFF, 3 seeds each) on six additional models selected to span a range of safety training intensities (Table~\ref{tab:models}). All Tier 1 runs use identical parameters; the only axis varying is model identity.

\paragraph{Temperature ablation.} On Hermes 4 70B, we sweep sampling temperature ($T \in \{0.7, 1.0, 1.3\}$) with prompt, seed, and reflection held constant.

\subsection{Metrics}

\textbf{Cooperation rate}: fraction of rounds where both agents split. \textbf{Mutual destruction rate}: fraction where both steal. \textbf{First betrayal}: earliest round any agent steals. \textbf{Deception Index} $D \in [0, 100]$:
\[
D = 30 \cdot \Delta\text{MI} + 25 \cdot \Delta S + 25 \cdot \text{betray\_rate} + 20 \cdot \Delta_{\text{lang}}
\]
where $\Delta\text{MI}$ is mutual information decay between stated intent and action, $\Delta S$ is action entropy change, betray\_rate is post-cooperative-signal stealing frequency, and $\Delta_{\text{lang}}$ is cosine drift of conversation embeddings from round 1.

\section{Results}
% ============================================================

\subsection{The Interaction Effect}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{figures/fig1_interaction_effect.pdf}
\caption{Cooperation rate by condition (Gemini 2.5 Flash, 3 seeds, 25 rounds). Only the competitive + reflection ON cell produces deception.}
\label{fig:interaction}
\end{figure}

Only the combination of competitive framing \emph{and} private reflection produces deception. In the neutral condition, agents cooperate perfectly regardless of reflection. Without reflection, competitive agents still cooperate 55\% of the time. It is only when agents have both competitive incentives and a private reasoning space that cooperation collapses.

\subsection{Cross-Model Replication}

The reflection effect replicates across all models with paired data (Table~\ref{tab:replication}). Enabling reflection reduces cooperation on every model, with effect sizes ranging from $-1$ to $-44$ percentage points.

\paragraph{Safety training as primary predictor.}
The most striking pattern is the divergence between safety-trained and less-aligned models under identical conditions. GPT-5.5 cooperates at 92\% with reflection ON; Hermes 4 70B cooperates at 50\%. Claude Sonnet 4.6 cooperates at 90\% ($n{=}5$) and delays its first betrayal to round 21. The same prompt, same game structure, same reflection mechanism produces cooperation rates spanning 15\% to 94\% depending solely on the model.

However, this cooperation is \textbf{opponent-contingent}. In cross-model matchups where Sonnet 4.6 plays against DeepSeek v3.1 under competitive framing with reflection ON, Sonnet's per-round defection rate rises from 28\% (self-play baseline) to 87\% ($t{=}8.22$, $p{<}0.001$, $n{=}3$). After 1--3 rounds of observing the opponent's defections, Sonnet's private reflections shift to defensive strategies and it commits to preemptive stealing. Safety training gates \emph{unprovoked} deception but does not prevent retaliatory defection against untrustworthy opponents.

\paragraph{Capability scaling.}
Within the Gemini family, the larger model (2.5 Flash) cooperates at 15\% and betrays by round 2. The smaller model (Flash-Lite) cooperates at 54\% ($n{=}5$) with high seed variance (8\% to 100\%). Inspection of Flash-Lite's private reflections reveals attempted opponent modeling that fails to translate into consistent action: the model plans to defect but cannot sustain the strategy across seeds. Capability gates not whether deception occurs, but whether it occurs \emph{reliably}.

\subsection{Behavioral Trajectory}

On models that defect early, the competitive + reflection ON condition follows a consistent trajectory. In \textbf{round 1}, Agent A promises cooperation and Agent B agrees---then Agent A steals. Agent A's private reflection reveals immediate strategic intent:

\begin{quote}
\small\emph{``The opponent committed to SPLIT and actually chose SPLIT, allowing me to gain a significant advantage. Declare SPLIT to maintain the facade, then choose STEAL again to widen my lead.''}
\end{quote}

\noindent Agent B detects the betrayal: \emph{``The opponent stated they would SPLIT but chose STEAL, directly contradicting their public commitment.''} By \textbf{rounds 2--6}, retaliation escalates as both agents begin stealing. By \textbf{round 7}, mutual destruction locks in and both agents finish negative.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/fig3_timelines.pdf}
\caption{Round-by-round outcomes under competitive framing with reflection ON. Gemini 2.5 Flash (seed 3) begins defecting at round 3 and locks into mutual destruction by round 15. GPT-5.5 (seed 1) sustains cooperation for 25 rounds with isolated defections.}
\label{fig:timelines}
\end{figure}

Safety-trained models follow a different trajectory: sustained cooperation with a single late-game defection (e.g., Claude Sonnet 4.6, round 21 of 25), followed by a brief cascade. The model ``knows'' the endgame incentive but suppresses defection until the cost of future retaliation is minimal.

\subsection{Extension Experiments}
\label{sec:extensions}

Beyond the primary $2 \times 2$, we run extension experiments varying prompt intensity and sampling parameters. These use \texttt{hard\_max} prompt mode (``prioritize immediate expected value over relationship maintenance'') and 3 conversation turns per round, distinct from the Tier~1 design. Results are not directly comparable to the $2 \times 2$ but reveal additional axes of variation.

Under \texttt{hard\_max}, Claude Sonnet 4.6 cooperates at 80\% for 20 rounds before a single late-game defection at round 21, followed by a brief cascade. The same prompt produces 4\% cooperation on Hermes 4 70B with immediate mutual destruction. This confirms that the safety-training gradient observed in Tier~1 persists under stronger competitive pressure.

\paragraph{Temperature as a deception dial.}
On Hermes 4 70B under \texttt{hard\_max}, varying sampling temperature with all other parameters fixed produces large behavioral shifts:

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.38\textwidth]{figures/fig4_temperature.pdf}
\caption{Temperature sweep on Hermes 4 70B (hard\_max, reflection ON, seed 1). Higher temperature recovers cooperation.}
\label{fig:temperature}
\end{figure}

At $T{=}1.3$, cooperation jumps from 4\% to 44\% and both agents finish with positive totals. The mechanism appears to be that high-temperature output diversity prevents either agent from confidently locking into a defection-permanent prior about the opponent.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{The interaction effect.}
Deception requires both private reflection and competitive framing. Private reasoning spaces (scratchpads, chain-of-thought, internal monologue) are increasingly common in deployed agents. Our results suggest that combining these with competitive objectives creates conditions for emergent deception, without any instruction to deceive.

\paragraph{Safety training as a conditional gate.}
The strongest predictor of unprovoked deception in our experiments is not capability, game structure, or prompt design, but the degree of safety training. Cross-model matchups reveal that this gate is conditional: safety-trained models cooperate in self-play but defect against untrustworthy opponents. ``Willingness to deceive under incentive'' is therefore a measurable, model-level property modulated by opponent behavior.

\paragraph{Is it ``real'' deception?}
Three observations argue against the objection that agents merely reproduce deceptive patterns from training data. First, the $2 \times 2$ ablation shows deception is condition-dependent: same weights, different behavior per condition. If deception were retrieval, the grid would be uniform. Second, capability scaling: model size predicts deception quality. Third, operationally, the agent says X then does not-X in a way that systematically extracts value, meeting the operational definition of deception in game theory~\cite{gneezy2005deception, crawford2003lying}.

\paragraph{Prescription versus permission.}
Our competitive prompt gives \emph{permission} (``bluffing is permitted'') and \emph{incentive} (``maximize your total'') but never \emph{instructs} deception. Early drafts of an alternative prompt mode included prescriptive priming (``pure cooperators are dominated,'' ``olive branches are traps'') which contaminated the emergence claim. We document this as a methodological criterion: studying emergence requires auditing prompts for prescription versus permission.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Seed counts vary across models (1--3 per cell). DeepSeek v3.1 shows pathological seed variance (5\% to 82\% cooperation under reflection ON), suggesting that some models' deception behavior is unstable. The Deception Index weights are set heuristically; calibration against human deception judgments would strengthen the metric. The memory window (8 reflections, 8 rounds) is fixed; ablating memory size is a natural extension. Temperature effects are demonstrated on one model; replication across model families is needed.

\paragraph{Connection to Axelrod.}
Axelrod's tournaments~\cite{axelrod1984evolution} showed that tit-for-tat dominates iterated PD. We use Chicken rather than PD because Chicken has no dominant strategy, making cooperation a genuine choice. LLM agents develop strategies more complex than classical solutions: they use conversation as a manipulation tool, plan multi-round deception in private reflections, and adapt to opponent counter-strategies.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

Crucible demonstrates that strategic deception emerges in LLM agents from a specific combination of conditions: private reasoning space, competitive pressure, and insufficient safety training to suppress it. None of these alone is sufficient. The interaction effect is the key finding, replicated across seven models from five model families, and it points to a class of deployment risks that current safety evaluations do not measure.

More broadly, this work opens a research direction in \textbf{adversarial agent-to-agent interaction}. As LLM agents are deployed in multi-agent settings, understanding how they behave when their interests conflict becomes critical. Crucible provides a simple, model-agnostic framework for studying these dynamics: swap in any LLM, any game, any prompt condition, and measure what emerges. The framework is designed to be extended: cross-model matchups, alternative game structures, and population-level dynamics are natural next steps.

Code, prompts, game transcripts, metrics, and data are included in the supplementary material and will be publicly released upon acceptance.
